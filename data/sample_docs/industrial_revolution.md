# Industrial Revolution in Britain

The Industrial Revolution began in Britain through several reinforcing factors rather than one single cause. Britain had accessible coal fields, iron making skill, ports, trade networks, and capital that could finance machines and factories.

Coal mattered because it supplied dense energy near growing industrial towns. The steam engine turned coal into mechanical power for mines, textile mills, transport, and later railways. Textile industry demand encouraged machines that spun and wove cotton faster than household labor.

Agriculture also changed. Enclosure and improved farming increased food output and pushed some rural labor toward towns. Trade and empire expanded markets, while ports and canals improved transport. Capital, coal, skilled mechanics, textile industry demand, labor movement, and transport formed a connected system around Britain.
